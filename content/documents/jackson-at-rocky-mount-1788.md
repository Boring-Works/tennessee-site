## Andrew Jackson at Rocky Mount, 1788

In the spring of 1788, a young attorney named Andrew Jackson crossed the Appalachian Mountains seeking opportunity on the frontier. He had been admitted to the North Carolina bar in September 1787 after studying law under Spruce Macay and John Stokes in Salisbury.

<passage id="six-weeks">
Andrew Jackson lodged at Rocky Mount for six weeks while waiting for his license to practice law in nearby Jonesborough. At twenty-one years old, he was already a licensed attorney, but needed approval to practice in the Western District of North Carolina.
</passage>

<passage id="waiting-period">
Jackson spent this waiting period at William Cobb's substantial home, hunting, fishing, and preparing for his career on the frontier. The Cobb residence offered comfortable accommodations for travelers on the road between North Carolina and the western settlements.
</passage>

<passage id="journey-west">
After receiving his license, Jackson continued westward. He had been appointed prosecuting attorney for the Western District by his friend John McNairy, the newly appointed Superior Court Judge. By October 1788, Jackson arrived in Nashville to serve as district attorney of the Mero District.
</passage>

## Historical Context

Jackson's 1788 stay at Rocky Mount occurred two years before Governor William Blount arrived to establish the territorial capital. At this time, the region was still part of North Carolina's western lands—the Southwest Territory would not be created until 1790.

### Timeline

- **September 1787:** Jackson admitted to North Carolina bar
- **Spring 1788:** Arrives in Jonesborough via Wilderness Road
- **Spring 1788:** Lodges at Rocky Mount for six weeks
- **August 1788:** Famous incident challenging Colonel Waightstill Avery to a duel in Jonesborough
- **October 1788:** Arrives in Nashville as district attorney

### The Cobb Connection

William Cobb's home served as a natural stopping point for travelers. The substantial two-story log structure—later noted by Governor Blount for its "Glass Windows"—offered comfort and safety on the frontier road. Jackson was among many notable figures who passed through Rocky Mount in its early years.

### Jackson's Later Career

The young lawyer who lodged at Rocky Mount would go on to serve as:

- Tennessee's first U.S. Representative (1796)
- U.S. Senator from Tennessee (1797-1798, 1823-1825)
- Justice of the Tennessee Supreme Court (1798-1804)
- Major General in the War of 1812
- 7th President of the United States (1829-1837)
