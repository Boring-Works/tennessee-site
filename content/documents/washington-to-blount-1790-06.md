## Letter from President Washington to William Blount

_June 12, 1790_

Sir,

The Senate having advised and consented to your appointment as Governor of the territory of the United States south of the river Ohio, I take this opportunity to communicate some instructions for your guidance.

<passage id="primary-objects">The primary objects of your attention should be the establishment of civil government, the cultivation of friendly relations with the Indian tribes, and the prevention of hostilities between the Indians and the settlers. These objects are closely connected, and success in one will promote success in the others.</passage>

<passage id="indian-policy">With respect to the Indians, you will endeavor by all proper means to cultivate their friendship. You will assure them of the just and humane intentions of the federal government, and you will carefully prevent encroachments on their lands by the settlers. A general treaty, establishing definite boundaries, should be negotiated as soon as circumstances permit.</passage>

<passage id="law-enforcement">You will enforce the laws of the United States with firmness but with moderation. The inhabitants of the frontier must understand that federal authority is supreme, but they must also perceive that it is exercised for their benefit and protection.</passage>

I am, with great esteem, your most obedient servant,

G. Washington
