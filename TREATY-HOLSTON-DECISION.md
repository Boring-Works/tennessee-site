# Treaty of Holston at Rocky Mount - Final Decision

**Research Lead:** Dr. Michael Redbird, PhD
**Date:** January 30, 2026
**Subject:** Resolution of conflicting claims about Rocky Mount's role in the Treaty of Holston

---

## Primary Source Evidence

### 1. Treaty Document Location (Where Signed)

**Source:** Treaty of Holston, July 2, 1791 (Avalon Project, Yale Law School; DigiTreaties; War Department Papers)

**Finding:** The treaty was signed "at the treaty ground on the bank of the Holston River, near the mouth of the French Broad, within the Southwest Territory."

This location is White's Fort (present-day Knoxville), NOT Rocky Mount.

### 2. Blount Correspondence (What He Wrote, From Where)

**Source 1:** Blount to Knox, December 15, 1790 (`blount-to-knox-1790-12.md`)

> "Several Cherokee chiefs, including the principal chief known as the Hanging Maw, have visited me at Rocky Mount. They express a desire for peace and appear sincere in their professions of friendship toward the United States."

**Significance:** Confirms Cherokee chiefs visited Rocky Mount in December 1790 for preliminary diplomatic discussions BEFORE the formal treaty negotiations.

**Source 2:** Blount to Knox, June 15, 1791 (`blount-to-knox-1791-06.md`)

> "The Cherokee chiefs are assembling at White's Fort for the proposed treaty. Already more than thirty have arrived, including the Hanging Maw, John Watts, and the warrior known as Doublehead."

**Significance:** DEFINITIVELY confirms that formal treaty negotiations took place at White's Fort, not Rocky Mount.

### 3. Cherokee Sources

All Cherokee signatory biographies in the repository confirm:

- **William Blount file:** "Meeting at White's Fort (present-day Knoxville), Blount secured the signatures of forty-two Cherokee chiefs"
- **Long Will file:** "In July 1791, Long Will participated in the Treaty of Holston negotiations at White's Fort"
- **The Boots file:** "In July 1791, The Boots traveled to White's Fort to participate in treaty negotiations"
- **Standing Turkey, Bear at Home:** Similar statements confirming White's Fort location

**Critical Note:** No Cherokee-authored documents about the treaty negotiations have been identified in archives. All Cherokee perspectives are reconstructed from U.S. government records. Cherokee oral histories and tribal records may contain different perspectives.

## What Happened at Rocky Mount (VERIFIED)

1. **Preliminary diplomatic visits (December 1790):** Cherokee chiefs including Hanging Maw visited Blount at Rocky Mount for initial discussions about peace and a future treaty.

2. **Treaty promise made (December 1790):** Blount promised Cherokee leaders that "a formal treaty will be negotiated in the coming year."

3. **Diplomatic correspondence (1790-1791):** Blount conducted official correspondence with Secretary of War Knox about Cherokee relations from Rocky Mount.

4. **Hospitality extended:** Cherokee delegation received hospitality at Rocky Mount during preliminary visits (per protocol and multiple sources mentioning Mary Cobb's role).

5. **Capital function:** Rocky Mount served as the administrative headquarters of the Southwest Territory during the treaty preparation period.

---

## What Happened at White's Fort (VERIFIED)

1. **Chiefs assembled (June 1791):** "The Cherokee chiefs are assembling at White's Fort for the proposed treaty"

2. **Formal negotiations (Late June 1791):** Weeks of negotiation between Blount and Cherokee representatives

3. **Treaty signed (July 2, 1791):** 42 Cherokee chiefs and warriors signed the treaty "at the treaty ground on the bank of the Holston River"

---

## Certainty Level

**90% confident** in the following conclusions:

1. The Treaty of Holston was **NOT negotiated at Rocky Mount** - formal negotiations occurred at White's Fort
2. The Treaty was **signed at White's Fort** (treaty ground on Holston River)
3. Preliminary Cherokee diplomatic visits **DID occur at Rocky Mount** (December 1790)
4. Blount conducted treaty-related **correspondence from Rocky Mount**
5. The treaty **was promised** to Cherokee leaders at Rocky Mount (December 1790)

The 10% uncertainty relates to:

- Possible undiscovered Cherokee oral histories or records
- Whether any preliminary negotiating discussions occurred at Rocky Mount visits
- The exact nature of December 1790 meetings (purely diplomatic courtesy vs. substantive discussion)

---

## DECISION: Option A (Modified)

**Selected Option:** Option A - Negotiations at White's Fort, correspondence from Rocky Mount

**But with important refinement:** Rocky Mount had MORE connection to the treaty than just "correspondence" - preliminary diplomatic meetings with Cherokee chiefs occurred there.

## Recommended Text

### Primary Statement (for main narrative):

> "In December 1790, Cherokee chiefs including Hanging Maw visited Governor Blount at Rocky Mount, where he promised a formal treaty in the coming year. Six months later, those negotiations took place at White's Fort (present-day Knoxville), culminating in the Treaty of Holston on July 2, 1791."

### Alternative (shorter version for space-constrained contexts):

> "Cherokee leaders met with Governor Blount at Rocky Mount in 1790 to discuss peace. The formal Treaty of Holston was negotiated and signed at White's Fort (Knoxville) in July 1791."

## Cherokee Perspective Note

**Critical framing for respectful interpretation:**

1. **The Cherokee were NOT supplicants.** They came as representatives of a sovereign nation negotiating with the United States. Framing them as "visiting" or "arriving" to receive hospitality minimizes their political agency.

2. **The treaty was adversarial.** Cherokee were negotiating to protect their lands from continued American encroachment. This was not a celebration for them - it was a necessary accommodation to prevent worse outcomes.

3. **Cherokee skepticism was justified.** The war faction (John Watts, Bloody Fellow, Doublehead) signed despite deep reservations. Within 6 months, Bloody Fellow led a delegation to Philadelphia to protest treaty terms. American treaty violations led to continued conflict until 1794.

4. **What Cherokee sources say:** Per biographies in the repository, "No Cherokee-authored documents about [these leaders] have been identified in archives. This biography reflects how he was perceived and documented by U.S. officials and settlers. Cherokee oral histories and tribal records may contain different perspectives."

5. **Avoid romanticizing:** Do not describe treaty events as "celebrations" or Cherokee presence as "honored guests." These were difficult negotiations under pressure, and Cherokee leaders knew the treaty would not fully protect their interests.

**Recommended framing:**

> "In December 1790, Cherokee leaders traveled to Rocky Mount to meet with Governor Blount and assess American intentions. Their willingness to negotiate reflected both a desire for peace and a realistic assessment that diplomacy offered better prospects than continued warfare against the expanding American settlements."

## Summary

**Rocky Mount's ACTUAL role in the Treaty of Holston:**

1. Site of preliminary Cherokee diplomatic visits (December 1790)
2. Where Blount promised a formal treaty
3. Administrative headquarters during treaty planning
4. Site of treaty-related correspondence

**Rocky Mount's CLAIMED but INCORRECT role:**

1. Site of treaty negotiations (FALSE - White's Fort)
2. Site of treaty signing (FALSE - Holston River treaty ground)
3. Site of celebrations (UNVERIFIED)

**The historically defensible narrative:**
Rocky Mount was where the treaty was _promised_, not where it was _negotiated_. Cherokee leaders met Blount at Rocky Mount in December 1790 and secured his commitment to a formal treaty. That treaty was negotiated and signed at White's Fort seven months later.
